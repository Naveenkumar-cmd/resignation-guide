The 90-Day Bridge Strategy:
Job to Business, Safely

Don't quit your job to start a business. Build the business first, then quit with a plan. Here's how.

The Romantic Myth of the Leap

There's a compelling cultural narrative around entrepreneurship that goes something like: "I took the leap, burned the boats, and committed fully." It sounds bold. It sounds decisive. And for a very small number of people with very specific circumstances, it works.

For the vast majority, quitting your job before your business has any validation, revenue, or momentum is a high-risk gamble that adds enormous financial pressure to an already difficult undertaking. Pressure rarely produces creativity. It produces survival mode.

The most successful founders we've spoken to didn't quit impulsively. They built quietly, validated thoroughly, and left when the business was ready to support them — not when their emotions peaked.

The 90-Day Framework Overview

The 90-Day Bridge Strategy is a structured approach to building the foundations of a viable business while still employed — using evenings, weekends, and your existing network — so that when you do leave, you're leaving toward something real, not toward a hope.

It breaks into three 30-day phases, each with specific deliverables. At the end of 90 days, you'll have enough information and early signals to make an informed, rational decision about whether and when to quit.

Days 1–30: Validate the Idea

The first 30 days are exclusively about testing assumptions, not building a product. The goal is to get to your first paying customer or your first credible signal of demand — without writing a single line of code or building a single deliverable.

  • Define your target customer with extreme specificity. Not "small businesses" — "founder-led B2B SaaS companies in India with 5–20 employees"
  • Have 10 real conversations with potential customers. Not surveys — conversations. Ask about their problems, not your solution.
  • Offer to solve their problem manually (a "concierge MVP") before you automate or productise anything
  • Target: at least 2–3 people who say "I would pay for this" — and then actually pay for it, even at a token amount

If you can't get 2–3 paying customers or committed early adopters in 30 days, that's valuable information — the idea as currently framed may need significant rethinking before you invest further.

Days 31–60: Build the Minimum

With validated demand, days 31–60 are about building the smallest possible version of your offering that you can charge for reliably. Not the product you envision eventually — the product that solves the specific problem your first customers articulated.

  • Set a strict scope: what is the single most important thing your product/service must do? Build only that.
  • Deliver manually to your first customers if needed — operational efficiency comes later. Prove that customers get value and return for more.
  • Collect testimonials, case studies, and referrals from every early customer
  • Begin building a small waitlist or email list of interested prospects through content or direct outreach
  • Target: ₹20,000–₹50,000 in revenue or committed contracts by day 60

Days 61–90: Establish Repeatability

The question days 61–90 must answer is: can you consistently acquire new customers, or were your first few customers a lucky personal network effect? Repeatability is what separates a business from a one-time project.

  • Identify your one most reliable customer acquisition channel and double down on it
  • Document your delivery process so it doesn't depend entirely on your personal time for every step
  • Build a 6-month financial model: what revenue do you need to replace your salary, and what's the realistic path to get there?
  • Talk to a CA about your business structure, GST registration, and tax planning
  • Target: a clear line-of-sight to ₹1–2L/month in revenue within 3–6 months of going full-time

The Quit Signal: When Is It Actually Time?

After 90 days, you should be able to answer these questions honestly. If the answers are yes, the conditions for quitting are forming:

  • Have you earned at least ₹50,000 from your business in the last 30 days?
  • Do you have at least 3–4 months of expenses saved as runway?
  • Do you have at least 2–3 recurring or repeat customers (not just one-offs)?
  • Do you have a clear, repeatable channel for acquiring new customers?
  • Has your business survived without you being present for at least a few days (i.e., it's not entirely dependent on your daily labour)?

If most of these are yes, the bridge is built. Now you can cross it with eyes open rather than jump and hope you can fly.
